\begin{abstract}
Deep learning models for Encrypted Traffic Classification (ETC) often achieve near-perfect performance in controlled settings but collapse during real-world deployment. Earlier works have tried to address this issue by using Domain Adaptation (UDA) and Continual Test-Time Adaptation (CTTA) methods. These methods, however, assume the degradation is caused by continuous, gradual, and homogeneous drift. In this paper, we expose the continuous drift fallacy, demonstrating that structural degradation in ETC ecosystems actually consists of discrete, asynchronous per-class covariate shifts---class "teleportations". As a result, global adaptation methods---gradient test-time adaptation (TENT, CoTTA) and adversarial UDA (DANN)---fail to help and often induce negative transfer. To address this, we reframe drift monitoring on a deployed, frozen model as inference-time open-set detection of known-but-drifted classes. We introduce a robust, label-free per-class monitor based on a Black Box Shift Estimation (BBSE)-corrected entropy residual. The monitor preserves accurate tracking of the true $F_1$ score ($\rho=-0.988$) while isolating genuine structural degradation from benign viral volume spikes. At 95\% detection it cuts the false-alarm rate to 4.6\%---a 5.4$\times$ reduction versus uncorrected entropy (25.0\%) and 14$\times$ versus an MFWDD-style global monitor (64.8\%)---with the same ordering reproduced in a clean-regime replication that excludes the dataset's one sensor event. Because the monitor identifies \emph{which} class degraded, it enables a targeted, label-free diagnose-and-repair proof of concept: recomputing only the flagged class's prototype restores it while leaving the stable majority untouched.
\end{abstract}

Modern encrypted-traffic classification (ETC) must infer an application from its
traffic behavior rather than its payload. As TLS~1.3 and the Encrypted
ClientHello (ECH) extension close the last cleartext handshake fields that
classic deep-packet inspection relied on, classifying encrypted traffic forces
operators onto side-channel models that learn from packet timing, direction, and
size sequences rather than from payload content
\cite{shamsimukhametov2024ech,lin2022etbert,luxemburk2023tls,seddigh2019framework}. These models
report near-perfect accuracy in the lab, yet a well-documented lab-to-production
rift degrades that performance once they are deployed against live, evolving
traffic --- the same temporal-bias and realism gap (concept drift,
network-condition variation, label scarcity) flagged across
malware and encrypted-traffic analysis more broadly
\cite{pendlebury2019tesseract,bahramali2023netclr}. The prevailing response
treats this erosion as a slow, global concept drift and counters it with
unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) or continual test-time adaptation (CTTA)
--- methods that assume the class boundaries move together, gradually, and in the
same direction \cite{wang2020tent,wang2022cotta,ganin2016dann,li2016adabn}.

We argue this picture is wrong, and that the misdiagnosis is precisely why
adaptation degrades performance instead of restoring it. The literature documents at least
three distinct failure modes --- slow accuracy decay \cite{malekghaini2022data},
abrupt collapses triggered by backend or version updates \cite{chen2025drift},
and outright generalization failures \cite{shamsimukhametov2024ech} --- but
treats them as points on a single continuous-drift spectrum. We call this the
\emph{Continuous Drift Fallacy}: the smooth macroscopic
accuracy decay that motivates global adaptation is not a coherent drift of the
whole feature space but a \emph{superposition} of discrete, asynchronous,
per-class steps. Individual applications hold their learned signature for many
weeks and then \emph{teleport} across empty latent space at their own,
vendor-driven moment (Fig.~\ref{fig:hero}, Fig.~\ref{fig:density})
\cite{jancicka2024noms}, while the stable majority of classes do not move. This
asymmetry predicts trouble for global adaptation: when only a handful of classes
have moved, an update driven by the aggregate target distribution chases those few
while distorting the many that were never broken. Consistent with this, we measure
net-negative transfer from global test-time adaptation
(Sec.~\ref{sec:negtransfer}), where an AdaBN control localizes the damage to the
global parameter update itself.

This observation lets us reframe the problem. If the deployed model is held
\emph{frozen}, a class that teleports does not become a new class; it becomes a
\emph{known} class whose flows now fall outside the model's learned decision
region. Monitoring drift on a frozen model is therefore closer to inference-time
open-set detection of known-but-drifted classes than to domain adaptation
\cite{bendale2016openmax}. The operational stakes favor this view: as ECH
deployment broadens and the per-class breaks keep arriving asynchronously, a
full supervised retrain at every event is prohibitive --- it pays a fresh
labeling campaign across the whole service catalogue and risks re-incurring the
negative transfer above. The pragmatic loop is to identify \emph{which} classes
jumped, recapture samples for only those classes, and apply a targeted update.
A label-free per-class monitor is exactly what makes that loop actionable.

We study these effects on CESNET-TLS-Year22 \cite{hynek2024cesnet}, a
year-spanning backbone dataset, and make four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{The continuous-drift fallacy.} We give direct empirical evidence
        that the smooth accuracy decay of a frozen ETC model is an illusion: it
        decomposes into one documented sensor step plus a long tail of discrete,
        asynchronous per-class \emph{teleportations}, and a label-vs-covariate
        decomposition pins the teleportations on covariate shift, not on benign
        label (volume) shift (Sec.~\ref{sec:discrete}).
  \item \textbf{Global adaptation does not help here.} Gradient test-time
        adaptation (TENT, CoTTA) induces negative transfer and even a best-case
        adversarial UDA model (DANN) buys no forward benefit
        (Sec.~\ref{sec:negtransfer}).
  \item \textbf{A label-free monitor that survives label shift.} We introduce a
        BBSE-corrected entropy residual that tracks hidden degradation while
        ignoring benign volume swings, and pinpoints \emph{which} class broke
        (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}--\ref{sec:eval}).
  \item \textbf{A diagnose-and-repair loop.} The monitor's per-class flag drives a
        targeted, label-free repair that restores broken classes without
        disturbing the stable majority (Sec.~\ref{sec:operational}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Test-Time Adaptation and UDA}
The canonical responses to distribution shift in deployed models are
domain-adversarial feature alignment (DANN \cite{ganin2016dann}), entropy-based
test-time adaptation (TENT \cite{wang2020tent}), and its continual variant with
consistency and stochastic restoration (CoTTA \cite{wang2022cotta}). What unites
these methods is that they are \emph{global}: they update shared parameters (BN
affine terms, all weights, or the feature extractor) to align the entire
target distribution to the source \cite{liang2024ttasurvey}. This is appropriate
when class boundaries drift together, but mis-specified when only a handful of
classes have moved --- the negative-transfer mechanism we quantify in
Sec.~\ref{sec:negtransfer}. Error accumulation and negative transfer under
non-i.i.d.\ test streams are in fact documented failure modes of this family
\cite{liang2024ttasurvey}. Compounding the problem, TTA and CTTA are rarely
evaluated on encrypted traffic at all, so their behavior in this regime is largely
undocumented; we supply that benchmark in Sec.~\ref{sec:negtransfer}.

\subsection{Unsupervised Drift Monitoring Frameworks}
Classical streaming concept-drift detectors --- error-rate monitors such as DDM
\cite{gama2004ddm} and windowing detectors such as ADWIN \cite{bifet2007adwin} ---
raise a single global alarm of \emph{when} a stream has changed, but are either
label-dependent (they watch an error stream) or class-agnostic, so they cannot say
\emph{which} class drifted. The closest prior art in our label-free, encrypted-traffic
setting is MFWDD \cite{mfwdd2024}, a model-based feature-weight
drift detector demonstrated on TLS and QUIC traffic, together with the recent
CESNET dataset-stability benchmark \cite{cesnetdrift2025}. Two contrasts
motivate our work. First, MFWDD's label-free mode is a \emph{global} trigger
that attributes drift to \emph{features}, not classes; per-class attribution in
that framework requires labels. Second, the feature-distribution distances such
detectors rely on also move under \emph{benign} label shift --- a viral volume
spike on one application shifts the marginal feature distribution without any
loss of accuracy --- so a feature-distance trigger cannot tell a structural break
from a benign surge. Our signal is instead per-class, label-free,
\emph{label-shift-corrected}, and calibrated against hidden $F_1$; we benchmark
it head-to-head against an MFWDD-style global detector in
Sec.~\ref{sec:robustness} and Sec.~\ref{sec:isolation}.\footnote{Our MFWDD-style
detector is a reimplementation in the \emph{spirit} of MFWDD --- a
feature-importance-weighted global distance --- computed on the model's per-class
output (softmax) distribution rather than on raw input features. We benchmark the
global feature-distance \emph{idea}, not the original system; the softmax-space
variant is, if anything, the more favorable one, since a raw input-feature
distance fires at least as hard under a benign volume spike.} The two answer different
questions: MFWDD identifies \emph{which feature drifted} (useful for dataset
curation), whereas our monitor identifies \emph{which class degraded} (the input
to a targeted repair). Adjacent encrypted-traffic work on website and mobile-app
fingerprinting has also met drift, but frames it as a training problem to be made
robust: learning drift-invariant representations \cite{jiang2022fgnet},
augmenting traces for realism-robust training \cite{bahramali2023netclr}, or
updating a single per-website classifier instead of retraining the whole model
\cite{deng2023ares}. None of them offers a label-free monitor that says which
class has degraded --- the gap this paper fills.

\subsection{Label-Shift Quantification}
Our monitor builds on the label-shift estimation literature: Black Box Shift
Estimation (BBSE) \cite{lipton2018bbse}, the EM-style prior-adjustment of
Saerens \emph{et al.} (SLD-EM) \cite{saerens2002sld}, regularized learning under
label shift (RLLS) \cite{azizzadenesheli2019rlls}, the unified
maximum-likelihood view (MLLS) \cite{garg2020mlls}, its bias-corrected
calibrated variant (MLLS+BCTS) \cite{alexandari2020mlls}, and Bayesian black-box
quantification \cite{ziegler2023bayesian}. Estimating class prevalence from
unlabeled data is the \emph{quantification-learning} task studied in its own right
\cite{gonzalez2017quant}. Every one of these
estimators rests on the same assumption that the class-conditional $P(X\mid Y)$
is invariant between source and target. A covariate shift violates the assumption
all of them depend on, biasing them simultaneously --- and, as we show empirically
(Sec.~\ref{sec:breakdown}), their errors spike \emph{together}. Where the label-shift
literature treats this as the failure case to avoid, we \emph{leverage} it: the
shared breakdown is the signal that distinguishes a genuine structural
break from a benign change in class proportions. The online-label-shift
setting --- where $P(y)$ drifts over time while $P(x\mid y)$ stays fixed --- is the
mirror image of ours, and we use it only as a contrast and as future work
(Sec.~\ref{sec:conclusion}); in our setting it is the covariate shift that breaks
the model and must be detected (Sec.~\ref{sec:decompose}).

\subsection{Open-Set Recognition as a Monitoring Lens}
\label{sec:related_osr}
Open-set recognition (OSR) equips a classifier to reject inputs that fall
outside its learned decision regions \cite{bendale2016openmax}. We adopt its
framing, not its algorithms. When a deployed model is held frozen, a known
class that teleports falls outside the decision geometry the model learned for
it, so at inference those flows behave like out-of-region or ``unknown''
samples. Drift monitoring on a frozen model is therefore well described as
inference-time open-set detection, with one important difference from standard
OSR \cite{geng2021osr}: these are \emph{known} classes whose signatures drifted,
not novel classes appearing for the first time --- our setting is the
covariate-shift cousin of OSR, not novel-class rejection. We use this view as an
interpretive frame, not as an algorithmic contribution. For the same reason we do not run an OSR baseline
battery in the main monitoring evaluation --- our detector is scored against
hidden $F_1$ and compared against MFWDD-style and uncorrected-entropy detectors
--- though we do report a per-class energy/MSP out-of-distribution comparison in
Sec.~\ref{sec:robustness} to address the natural ``why not OOD methods'' question
directly. The closest prior framing to ours is HOLMES
\cite{deng2024holmes}, which already instantiates the per-class open-set view in
encrypted traffic with spatial decision regions, out-of-region rejection, and
even per-class region updates on drift. HOLMES, however, is supervised --- labels
define its regions --- and is a website-fingerprinting \emph{attack}. What
neither HOLMES nor any prior monitor addresses is what we target: separating
covariate-driven degradation from \emph{benign label shift}, and calibrating the
per-class signal against the model's hidden $F_1$. That, rather than per-class
localization alone, is our contribution; our monitor is also label-free, operates
on a frozen ETC model, and additionally quantifies the harm of global adaptation.

\section{The Nature of Discrete Drift in Encrypted Traffic}
\label{sec:discrete}

\subsection{Two Kinds of Discrete Drift}
\label{sec:modalities}
We study CESNET-TLS-Year22 \cite{hynek2024cesnet}, a year-spanning 2022 dataset
of TLS flows from a 100\,Gbps backbone, organized as 52 weekly snapshots
covering 180 services across 24 categories. Our backbone is the multimodal
mm-CESNET architecture --- a Conv1D branch over the per-packet (PPI) sequence
plus an MLP over flow statistics --- taken from the open-source cesnet-models
codebase \cite{luxemburk2023tls}. No source weights are released for this
dataset, so we train every source model ourselves.\footnote{The 96.3\%
next-week ($T{+}1$) accuracy reported for this dataset \cite{hynek2024cesnet} is
measured under the authors' own protocol; our numbers are 52-week
\emph{forward} averages of a frozen model under accumulating drift and are not
comparable to it. The comparable reference is our own in-distribution
accuracy, 0.845 at Week-1 and 0.883 at Week-16.}

The macroscopic accuracy curve of a frozen source model decaying over the year
looks like continuous drift, but it resolves into two qualitatively different
modalities. \textbf{Modality~1} is a single documented measurement-infrastructure
event at Week~10: a 9~March~2022 ipfixprobe exporter upgrade that began skipping
retransmitted packets, coinciding with post-invasion DDoS conditions
\cite{hynek2024cesnet}. This is not application drift but a sensor change, and we
repurpose it as a \emph{ground-truth} covariate event; the MFWDD detector, run on
the same dataset, also flagged this period, triggering two of its retrainings and
attributing them to the monitoring change \cite{mfwdd2024}. \textbf{Modality~2} is
the asynchronous application \emph{teleportation} that accounts for the remaining
year-long decay: genuine,
vendor-driven, per-class step changes in which one application's signature jumps
across empty latent space while its neighbors stay put
(Figs.~\ref{fig:hero},~\ref{fig:density}). This is not a CESNET-specific artifact
--- per-application version updates are known to drive per-class signature shifts
in mobile-app fingerprinting \cite{jiang2022fgnet}, and the same teleportation
reproduces on an independent, proprietary closed-world dataset (anonymized for
review; Sec.~\ref{sec:negtransfer}, Appendix
Fig.~\ref{fig:app_proprietary_tsne}).

A natural objection is that the vulnerable classes are simply long-tail and
under-trained. The \emph{timing} of their collapse rules this out. The
vulnerable classes hold their accuracy for many weeks and then step-drop at
\emph{different} weeks (Fig.~\ref{fig:classdrop}): an under-trained class would
be weak from Week~1, not fall sharply at Week~28. A static
$F_1$-versus-frequency scatter would only re-introduce the scarcity confound we
are dispelling --- a rare class can look weak for reasons unrelated to drift ---
so we rely on the \emph{timing} of each class's collapse instead, which scarcity
cannot explain.

We apply one reference-week rule throughout. Week-16 is our clean-regime
reference for all Modality-2 analyses: it sits in a healthy stretch of the year,
far enough past the Week-10 sensor transient for that transient to have settled
(the choice of week~16 specifically is not otherwise meaningful). Week-1 is used
only to expose and validate the documented Week-10 event (Modality~1) and,
because it alone spans a healthy-versus-degraded split, to anchor the primary
anomaly-detection benchmark, which we then replicate on Week-16
(Sec.~\ref{sec:robustness}). The bottom line of this section --- that the smooth
macro curve is a one-time sensor step plus a long tail of asynchronous per-class
steps, not continuous drift --- is established quantitatively in the sections
that follow.

\subsection{Decomposing Performance Degradation}
\label{sec:decompose}
Two very different things can move an application's traffic. A \emph{label shift}
is a change in how \emph{much} of each class we see --- a benign swing in volume
(a viral spike, a weekday/weekend cycle, a marketing campaign) that leaves each
class's traffic looking exactly as before. A \emph{covariate shift} is a change in
what the traffic \emph{looks like} for a fixed class --- a backend migration or
app-version update that alters its packet-size and timing signature. Only the
second kind actually breaks a frozen classifier, and it is the kind we want to
monitor; the first is benign noise the monitor must \emph{not} alarm on.

To separate the two we run a synthetic ablation
\cite{lipton2018bbse,saerens2002sld}. A \emph{label-only} curve keeps the
reference week's features but re-weights them to each later week's class
proportions, so it isolates the damage caused by label shift alone. A
\emph{covariate-only} curve does the opposite: it uses each week's real features
but holds the class proportions fixed, isolating the damage caused by covariate
shift alone. We run the ablation against both references
(Fig.~\ref{fig:decomp}). Under Week-1 (Fig.~\ref{fig:decomp}a) the Week-10 crash
is almost entirely covariate, with the label-only curve staying benign ---
confirming the documented sensor cause of Modality~1. Under Week-16
(Fig.~\ref{fig:decomp}b) the clean-regime teleportation degradation is
\emph{also} covariate-driven, while the label-only curve stays flat. The bottom
line: the degradation that matters is covariate, not label --- so a useful monitor
must track covariate breaks while deliberately ignoring benign label shift.

\subsection{Case Study: A Per-Class Signature Shift (eset-edtd)}
\label{sec:casestudy}
We single out the eset-edtd service as a worked example. Under the frozen Week-16
source its recall is stable through the healthy weeks and then collapses in a
single step at ISO week~17 (Fig.~\ref{fig:classdrop}), and the same jump is
visible in latent space (Fig.~\ref{fig:hero}d): the mean distance of its
embeddings from the Week-16 centroid is flat for several weeks and then steps up
sharply --- a discrete move, not a gradual drift. The shift is internal to the
encrypted signature: the week-17 jump is driven by the packet-size and direction
sequence rather than by flow-volume statistics, and a domain classifier separates
the pre- and post-cliff regimes almost perfectly (AUC~$0.92$). This is a discrete
change in the class's per-flow \emph{signature} (covariate shift), not a change in
the class's \emph{prevalence} (which is what benign label shift would be);
attribution details are in Appendix~\ref{app:shap}. We searched for a documented external cause and did not
find one: the only nearby ESET event, the spring-2022 EDTD$\to$LiveGuard rebrand,
left the underlying endpoints (edtd.eset.com) and certificates unchanged
and predates the cliff, so we report eset-edtd as an unexplained but internally
verified teleportation rather than attribute it to a specific event. A second
class, docker-registry, teleports at $\approx$Week~27 with the same character
(bulk-transfer features give way to lighter signaling) and likewise has no
documented external anchor; it serves as a purely mechanistic example.

\paragraph{Real-world correlates of the asynchrony.} Why would per-class breaks
arrive \emph{asynchronously}? The encrypted fingerprint of an application is set
by its client build and serving infrastructure, both of which churn on
\emph{independent} vendor schedules --- monthly browser releases (Chrome~98$\to$108
and Firefox~96$\to$108 over 2022), client app updates, and server-side HTTP/3
enablement (e.g.\ a major CDN switching on QUIC in August~2022) each reach
different applications in different weeks. Consistent with this, several of the
teleporting classes have a dated vendor client release landing within their cliff
week --- TeamViewer~15.31.5 (28~June, $\approx$wk26), Skype~8.86 (14~July,
$\approx$wk28), and Opera's 92.x weather-widget rework (early October,
$\approx$wk40) --- although the public release notes do not document a transport
change, so we read these as temporal coincidences consistent with vendor-driven
change, not proven causes. We deliberately do \emph{not} invoke Encrypted
ClientHello here: ECH was not deployed at scale until late~2023, after this
dataset. We also separate out a benign category: the late-December discontinuities
of a few classes (e.g.\ google-fonts, microsoft-weather) coincide with the
Czech academic backbone's Christmas/New-Year lull, which the dataset's authors
likewise attribute to vacation-driven volume rather than drift
\cite{hynek2024cesnet}; we treat these as holiday artifacts, not teleportations.
The takeaway is that the asynchronous, per-class step pattern is exactly the
footprint expected from uncoordinated vendor release and infrastructure cycles.

\section{Why Global Adaptation Fails: Negative Transfer}
\label{sec:negtransfer}
%  PROMOTED to its own section (was Sec V-D). This is the empirical anti-UDA leg.
%  SELF-CONTAINED: uses only the frozen model + standard adapters, NOT our residual.

A reviewer who accepts that drift is per-class might still ask: why not adapt
globally anyway? We test this directly. We take one source model, hold it frozen,
and evaluate it forward over every later week, comparing it against four standard
adapters: AdaBN \cite{li2016adabn}, which only recalibrates BN statistics and
performs no gradient update; TENT \cite{wang2020tent}, which updates the BN affine
parameters by entropy minimization; CoTTA \cite{wang2022cotta}, which updates all
parameters; and DANN \cite{ganin2016dann}, adversarial feature alignment. All
share the backbone, a 10\% sample, and seed~42, so any difference is attributable
to the adaptation mechanism. Because small batches inflate the BN-recalibration
effect, we report TENT and CoTTA at both batch~64 and batch~256, treating the
batch~256 (training-batch) figure as the fair operating point.

The merged results (Table~\ref{tab:nt_merged}) are consistent across datasets. The
gradient methods, TENT and CoTTA, are net-negative on both TLS source weeks and
on QUIC, and they fall furthest on the far, drifted weeks --- exactly where
adaptation is supposed to help. The magnitude depends on batch size: at batch~64
the collapse is severe, while at the fair batch~256 operating point the TLS effect
is mild ($-0.014$); the large deltas show up at small batch and, most sharply, on
the closed-world dataset ($-0.11$ to $-0.21$). Because the failure spans both the
entropy-based (TENT) and consistency-based (CoTTA) families, it points to the
global \emph{scope} of the update rather than to any one algorithm. The AdaBN
control makes the same point from the other side: recalibrating BN statistics with
no gradient step does no harm forward-only ($|\Delta|\le0.011$ on TLS, and a small
$+0.020$ on QUIC), so what hurts is the global \emph{parameter} update, not
adaptation in general. (An apparent $+0.007$ AdaBN ``gain'' at Week-16 is backward
transfer to the easy pre-source weeks and disappears forward-only.)

DANN \cite{ganin2016dann} differs from the test-time adapters above: it is a
\emph{trained} UDA method that learns a feature extractor adversarially so that source and target
become indistinguishable to a domain discriminator, which --- unlike the
test-time adapters above --- means retraining the model on each target week rather
than merely updating statistics at inference. We grant DANN the most favorable setting, scoring
each week with its own dedicated source-16$\to$week-$N$ model (transductive). Even so, DANN beats the frozen source by only $+0.006$ on
average ($+0.001$ on the far weeks; Table~\ref{tab:nt_merged}, per-week in
Fig.~\ref{fig:dann_curve}): global source--target alignment yields no forward
robustness and cannot recover per-class discrete drift, despite paying a full
retraining cost every week. The closed-world dataset shows the same neutral DANN
result ($+0.011$). We report DANN here for the Week-16 and closed-world sources
(its Week-1 and QUIC diagonals are still computing; the table marks those cells
pending). The gradient-method effects, by contrast, hold under both TLS source
weeks and on QUIC \cite{luxemburk2023quic} and are confirmed across five seeds, so
they are neither seed noise nor an artifact of the Week-10 sensor event. As
elsewhere, the 10\% sample makes these absolute accuracies non-comparable to the
full-data monitor figures.

\paragraph{External validation.} The same pattern reproduces on an independent,
proprietary closed-world dataset (anonymized for review). Evaluated forward-only
from a clean source week, its frozen model loses ground under every global
test-time adapter, with the penalty again falling on the stable majority; the
gradient methods are worse than on CESNET, and the effect is stable across five
seeds. As on CESNET, a from-scratch DANN model is essentially neutral, and the
discrete per-class teleportation itself reproduces (about a fifth of classes show
recall cliffs). The 10\% sample makes these absolute numbers non-comparable to the
full-data figures, so we report them as single-dataset external corroboration, not
a headline result.

\section{Methodology: Leveraging Estimator Failure}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Entropy as an Error Proxy}
The softmax output entropy of a classifier is a well-established per-sample error
proxy: incorrect predictions tend to land near the decision boundary and so carry
higher entropy than confident, correct ones \cite{wang2020tent}. Fig.~\ref{fig:entropy}
confirms this on our data, with the entropy of incorrect predictions clearly
shifted above that of correct ones. Entropy alone, however, cannot separate a
benign volume spike from a structural break: \textbf{a viral surge of an
inherently high-uncertainty application raises the mean entropy without any drop
in accuracy}, and a naive entropy monitor would mistake it for degradation.
Distinguishing the two requires a \emph{baseline} for how much entropy the current
class mix should produce --- which motivates the residual of
Sec.~\ref{sec:residual}, built directly from these per-sample entropies.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/entropy_is_indicative.png}}
\caption{Predictive entropy is an error proxy: on Week-16-source frozen inference
(forward weeks 17--52), misclassified flows carry markedly higher entropy than
correct ones (mean $1.54$ vs $0.35$ nats).}
\label{fig:entropy}
\end{figure}

\subsection{BBSE: Robust to Label Shift, Broken by Covariate Shift}
\label{sec:breakdown}
BBSE recovers the target label proportions from a frozen classifier's
predictions under the assumption that $P(X\mid Y)$ is invariant
\cite{lipton2018bbse}. That single assumption yields two opposite behaviors, and
the residual exploits the gap between them. In \textbf{Regime~1}
(Fig.~\ref{fig:regime1}, Week-16), where the shift is pure label shift and
$P(X\mid Y)$ stays intact, BBSE, RLLS, and EM-MLLS all hold a flat, low error
floor across injected severities $s\in[0,4]$ (60 draws per level, weeks 13--20),
while a static-prior baseline is roughly $11\times$ worse
\cite{lipton2018bbse,azizzadenesheli2019rlls}. This is the premise for the
$\Delta H_t\approx0$ branch of the residual: when the model has only seen a label
shift, the estimated proportions $\mathrm{w}_t$ are trustworthy. We note that
EM-MLLS is the \emph{most} accurate estimator in this regime, a point that becomes
important when we contrast estimation accuracy with detection robustness in
Sec.~\ref{sec:isolation}.\footnote{CESNET's dynamic per-service sampling flattens
label proportions, so the dataset-space shift understates the true network-space
shift; we therefore also re-project to approximate network proportions (the exact
ratios are unpublished). The same $\approx11\times$ gap and identical estimator
ordering hold in both spaces, so this sampling artifact does not affect the
covariate, per-class-$F_1$, or teleportation conclusions \cite{hynek2024cesnet}.}
In \textbf{Regime~2} (Fig.~\ref{fig:regime2}, Week-1), at the documented Week-10
covariate shift, the $L_1$ error of BBSE, RLLS, and SLD-EM \emph{spikes together}
\cite{lipton2018bbse,azizzadenesheli2019rlls,saerens2002sld} --- the premise for the
$\Delta H_t\gg0$ anomaly branch. The monitor itself uses a single estimator
(BBSE); that all of these estimators break \emph{together} at a covariate shift,
rather than any one breaking idiosyncratically, is what makes the breakdown a
trustworthy detector and not a noisy one.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/fig_estimation_robustness_severity_ref16_debiased.png}}
\caption{Regime~1 (Week-16): under pure \emph{label} shift --- class proportions
change but each class still looks the same ($P(X\mid Y)$ intact) --- the
label-shift estimators stay accurate at every severity ($x$-axis = size of the
injected shift), while a fixed-prior baseline degrades in step with it. This is
why the residual can trust the estimated proportions and stays near zero under
benign volume swings.}
\label{fig:regime1}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/fig_estimation_accuracy (1).png}}
\caption{Regime~2 (Week-1): at the documented Week-10 \emph{covariate} shift,
every label-shift estimator's error spikes at once --- the assumption they all
share ($P(X\mid Y)$ invariant) is violated together. This shared breakdown is the
signal the residual fires on.}
\label{fig:regime2}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The BBSE-Corrected Entropy Residual}
\label{sec:residual}
Let $h_{\mathrm{ref}}(c)=\mathbb{E}_x[\,H(p(x))\mid y=c\,]$ be the mean predictive
entropy of class $c$, estimated on the reference week, and let
$\bar H_t=\tfrac1N\sum_i H(p(x_i))$ be the mean per-sample predictive entropy over
window $t$ --- the same quantity whose error-proxy behavior Fig.~\ref{fig:entropy}
shows. A window's entropy should be judged not against a constant but against what
its \emph{current class mix} would produce. We therefore define the monitor's
signal as the residual between the observed mean entropy and this class-mix
baseline:
\[
  \Delta H_t \;=\; \bar H_t \;-\; \sum_{c}\mathrm{w}_t(c)\,h_{\mathrm{ref}}(c),
\]
where $\mathrm{w}_t$ are the BBSE-estimated current class proportions and the sum
runs over the classes present in the reference week. The anomaly score is
$\Delta H_t$ itself: larger values mean more degradation, hence its negative
correlation with $F_1$. The \emph{only} thing the correction changes is the
weights on a fixed per-class reference-entropy table --- the uncorrected baseline
uses the static source prior $p_{\mathrm{src}}(c)$ in place of $\mathrm{w}_t(c)$,
so it cannot adjust its expected entropy when the class mix shifts.

The mechanism follows from the two regimes of Sec.~\ref{sec:breakdown}. Under a
benign \emph{label} shift with $P(X\mid Y)$ intact, BBSE is consistent, so
$\mathrm{w}_t$ tracks the true mixture and the baseline rises in step with
$\bar H_t$; the two terms move together and \textbf{the residual stays near zero}
(the Regime-1 branch, Fig.~\ref{fig:regime1}). Under a \emph{covariate} shift the
broken class's flows no longer match the learned signature and produce diffuse,
high-entropy softmax. Empirically (Fig.~\ref{fig:regime2}) the observed
$\bar H_t$ then \emph{outruns} the reweighted baseline: the broken flows are more
uncertain than the reference entropy of any class their mass is reattributed to,
so however $\mathrm{w}_t$ is corrupted the baseline cannot absorb the rise, and
\textbf{the residual spikes} (the Regime-2 branch). The residual is thus a
two-sided instrument: deliberately blind to label shift, and sharply responsive
to the covariate breaks that signal genuine degradation.

\section{Experimental Evaluation of the Monitor}
\label{sec:eval}
%  CONSOLIDATED: mirror -> primary viral-spike -> isolation -> estimator sweep.

This section evaluates the monitor in four steps. We first check that the residual
\emph{tracks} the model's hidden accuracy with no labels
(Sec.~\ref{sec:mirror}). We then test the property that actually matters ---
\emph{robustness}: staying silent under benign volume swings while still catching
genuine degradation (Sec.~\ref{sec:robustness}). We show that the per-class flags
\emph{isolate} the broken classes without bleeding onto stable ones
(Sec.~\ref{sec:isolation}), and finally confirm the result does not hinge on the
particular label-shift estimator we plug in (Sec.~\ref{sec:sweep}).

\subsection{The Mirror Effect: Tracking Without Labels}
\label{sec:mirror}
Our first question is whether the residual tracks the model's hidden accuracy
without any labels. On clean forward data from the Week-16 source (weeks 17--52,
Fig.~\ref{fig:mirror}), it does, and so does its uncorrected counterpart: the
correlation between the entropy gap and the true Macro-$F_1$ is $\rho=-0.983$ for
the uncorrected gap, $-0.988$ for the BBSE-corrected residual, $-0.992$ for RLLS,
and $-0.991$ for an oracle that is handed the true proportions. BBSE correction
does \emph{not} improve clean tracking, nor is it meant to;
its purpose is robustness, established in the next subsection. Two further points
follow from this figure. First, the oracle line sits essentially on top of
BBSE and RLLS, so a better estimator could not track meaningfully better; clean
tracking is already near its ceiling. Second, SLD-EM \emph{fails to track}
entirely on this data ($\rho\approx0$) --- a striking failure that doubles as the visual evidence
for the estimation/detection dissociation we argue in Sec.~\ref{sec:sweep}. The
Week-1 mirror, which tracks the model \emph{through} the documented Week-10 event
at $\rho=-0.997$ (uncorrected) and $-0.994$ (BBSE), is deferred to Appendix
Fig.~\ref{fig:app_mirror_w1}. The per-class degradation grid
(Fig.~\ref{fig:grid}) makes the same point class by class: the top classes hold
and then drop in isolated step functions, and \textbf{the BBSE-estimated
proportions decouple from the true volume exactly when that class's $F_1$ drops}
--- a direct preview of why the residual fires.

\begin{figure}[htbp]

\subsection{Robustness Under Benign Volume Volatility}
\label{sec:robustness}
Correlation, as the previous subsection showed, is the wrong axis on which to
compare detectors: on clean data every detector achieves AUROC$\approx1.000$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:auroc}(a)). The discriminating axis is \emph{robustness} --- whether
a monitor alarms on genuine degradation while staying silent under benign volume
volatility. We are explicit about the trade this entails: on perfectly clean data
the uncorrected entropy is in fact \emph{sharper} than our residual (Sec.~\ref{sec:sweep}),
so the BBSE correction is an insurance policy with a measurable premium --- a few
points of clean-data AUROC given up to stay reliable once benign label shift
appears. The rest of this section quantifies both sides of that trade. We frame this as binary anomaly detection. The monitor sees only the
frozen model's unlabelled predictions, and a week is labeled anomalous iff its
true Macro-$F_1$ falls below 0.60. The ground truth is cause-agnostic: it includes
the Week-10 sensor event, which an operator would legitimately want flagged. The
benign stressor is a single-application viral spike, in which one application is
upsampled to a fraction $f\in[0.30,0.85]$ of an 8k-flow window while the way each
class's traffic actually looks (its $P(X\mid Y)$) is left untouched. Because the
spike never changes any class's true degradation status --- the spiked
application's own $F_1$ stays near 0.83 --- every alarm it provokes on a healthy
week is, by construction, a false alarm.

\paragraph{Two benign stressors.} A real viral surge is \emph{asymmetric}: a
single application balloons while the rest hold steady, and it usually lands on a
high-uncertainty class --- this is our realistic stressor. As an adversarial worst
case we also apply a \emph{symmetric} shift that perturbs every class's proportion
at once. The symmetric case is the easy one: all detectors stay well correlated
with the hidden $F_1$ and degrade only gradually as the shift grows
(Fig.~\ref{fig:symmetric}), with the corrected estimators degrading least. The
damaging stressor --- the one that drives a real wedge between corrected and
uncorrected detectors --- is the asymmetric single-app spike, which is what the
rest of this subsection uses.

\paragraph{Results.} On the Week-1 stream --- the one with enough degraded weeks to
form a healthy-vs-degraded split (43 degraded vs 9 healthy weeks) --- the
BBSE-corrected residual reaches AUROC 0.987 at a 4.6\% false-alarm rate under 95\%
matched detection, against 0.959 / 25.0\% for the uncorrected entropy gap and
0.677 / 64.8\% for the MFWDD-style global detector (Fig.~\ref{fig:auroc}(b),
Table~\ref{tab:auroc}). With only 9 healthy weeks these point false-alarm rates are
coarse, so we treat the consistent \emph{ordering} --- reproduced in the Week-16
replication and the block bootstrap below --- as the robust finding rather than the
exact $5.4\times$/$14\times$ multipliers. The mechanism is score-level: a viral spike inflates the
uncorrected entropy gap into the degradation band, whereas BBSE recognizes the
over-represented application, raises the expected entropy accordingly, and pulls
the residual back below threshold. The MFWDD-style detector trails the rest
because a single global feature distance cannot, by construction, separate a
benign surge from a structural break --- precisely the gap our correction is built
to close.

\paragraph{Week-16 replication.} To confirm the advantage is not an artifact of
the Week-10 sensor event, we repeat the experiment entirely within the clean
Week-16 forward regime (5 seeds, Fig.~\ref{fig:auroc_w16}). Here the clean regime
is trivial for every detector (AUROC$\approx1.000$, FAR$_{95}\approx0$), and under
the viral spike the corrected estimators again separate from the rest: BBSE-corrected
0.834, RLLS 0.849, MLLS+BCTS 0.827, all well above uncorrected 0.708 and MFWDD-style
0.650 (SLD-EM 0.759). The absolute AUROCs are lower than
on Week-1 because the Week-16 viral task is harder --- there is no large sensor
break inflating the positives --- but the ordering is unchanged, so the
correction's benefit is intrinsic to the asymmetric-spike stressor, not a
by-product of the sensor event.

\paragraph{Per-class OOD baselines, and why robustness is the right axis.} The
open-set framing raises an obvious alternative --- per-class
out-of-distribution (OOD) scores. We add the two standard ones --- a per-class
energy score $E=-\log\sum\exp(\text{logits})$ \cite{liu2020energy} and the
maximum-softmax-probability (MSP) baseline \cite{hendrycks2017msp} --- and score
them, alongside naive entropy and our residual, as we sweep the
amount of benign label shift present (Fig.~\ref{fig:oodsweep}). On perfectly clean
data the uncorrected scores are the \emph{sharpest}: naive entropy (AUROC 0.967)
and MSP (0.947) detect degraded classes best, ahead of the BBSE-corrected residual
(0.903) and energy (0.892). This clean-regime sharpness is exactly what the
correction trades away, and it would settle the comparison only if deployment data
were free of label shift. It is not. Even in CESNET's deliberately
imbalance-flattened release, per-class weekly volume routinely swings about
$5\times$ (median of the per-class max/min proportion), with the tail exceeding
$30\times$ and $100\times$ and individual services reaching thousand-fold; and
because CESNET re-tunes each service's sampling rate every five minutes by
prevalence --- compressing exactly the spikes we care about --- these figures are a
\emph{lower bound} on the true network swings. Benign label shift of
$10$--$50\times$ and beyond is therefore routine in deployment. Under it the OOD
scores behave like naive entropy: they decay and cross below the corrected residual
quickly --- energy and MSP fall below it by a severity of only $s\!\approx\!1$, naive
by $s\!\approx\!5$ --- and by $s\!=\!1000$ they have collapsed to $\approx0.70$ while
the residual still holds $\approx0.81$. The correction is what keeps the per-class
signal trustworthy under the label shift ETC deployments actually see.

\subsection{Per-Class Isolation}
\label{sec:isolation}
A monitor is only useful if it fires on the \emph{genuinely degraded} classes and
not on healthy classes that merely changed volume. We test this isolation property
on the Week-16 stream, and the pattern mirrors the sweep above. The uncorrected
entropy gap is sharper on clean data, but under injected benign label shift it
\emph{floods}: at $1000\times$ severity it raises alarms on up to $0.48$ of the
stable classes, whereas the BBSE-corrected residual keeps that bleed to
$0.18$--$0.27$ (the MFWDD-style detector barely fires at all). Equivalently, at
matched recall the corrected residual disturbs consistently fewer healthy classes
than either baseline. The per-panel ROC, bleed-versus-severity, and matched-recall
curves are collected in Appendix Fig.~\ref{fig:app_isopanels}. These isolated
per-class flags are exactly the input the open-set repair loop of
Sec.~\ref{sec:operational} consumes
\cite{tao2020fscil,rebuffi2017icarl,kirkpatrick2017ewc,li2017lwf,shahraki2022active}.

\subsection{Estimator-Variant Sweep and the Estimation/Detection Dissociation}
\label{sec:sweep}
Two questions remain: does the isolation result depend on the choice of
estimator, and what is the uncorrected detector's clean-data sharpness actually
worth? We answer both by sweeping the injected label-shift severity $s$ on the
Week-16 stream (Fig.~\ref{fig:sweep}). At the mildest injected severity $s{=}1$ the
uncorrected detector indeed leads (0.921 vs 0.897 for BBSE), but its AUROC decays steeply, is overtaken by
BBSE between $s{=}3$ and $s{=}5$, and collapses to $\approx0.70$ at $s{=}1000$; BBSE,
soft-BBSE, and RLLS all hold near 0.80, while EM/MLLS decays faster. The
uncorrected detector looks best only when there is nothing to be
robust to.

This exposes a clean \emph{estimation/detection dissociation}. EM/MLLS is the
\emph{most accurate} label-shift estimator in the robustness benchmark
(Fig.~\ref{fig:regime1}), yet it is the \emph{least robust} detector here; SLD-EM
even fails to track ($\rho\approx0$) on the Week-16 mirror (Fig.~\ref{fig:mirror}) and
ties the uncorrected false-alarm rate under a viral spike
(Table~\ref{tab:auroc}). Estimation accuracy and detection robustness are
therefore not the same objective in our experiments. In practice we keep plain
BBSE: soft-confusion BBSE is a near-equivalent swap, RLLS ties it, and EM/MLLS is
unsuitable for this use. Both this sweep and the isolation ablation run on Week-16,
keeping the clean-regime analyses internally consistent.

\section{Operational Paradigm: The Open-Set Monitoring View}
\label{sec:operational}
%  LIGHTENED (few-shot deferred). Ties isolation -> open-set lens -> next paper.
The monitor's per-class flag suggests a natural operational paradigm. Rather than
treat drift as a domain-adaptation problem, we recast it as targeted
class-incremental repair: when a class is flagged, we isolate its decision region
and treat it as an open-set region whose flows have fallen out of place
\cite{bendale2016openmax}. The isolation results of Sec.~\ref{sec:isolation} are
that region detector in practice. The repair is then a localized, few-shot
recapture for the one flagged class, leaving the stable domains untouched, rather
than a global labeling campaign
\cite{tao2020fscil,rebuffi2017icarl,kirkpatrick2017ewc,li2017lwf,shahraki2022active};
prior encrypted-traffic work already shows that such per-class or per-classifier
updates beat holistic retraining \cite{deng2023ares,deng2024holmes}.

This is also why a monitor that only says \emph{when} to retrain is not enough.
Knowing the moment of degradation still leaves the operator with one expensive
option --- a full supervised retrain across the entire service catalogue --- and
under ECH that option only gets worse: as ECH closes the remaining cleartext
handshake fields, the model leans harder on exactly the per-class signatures that
teleport, the breaks keep arriving asynchronously (Sec.~\ref{sec:intro}), and each
full retrain re-pays the whole labeling campaign \emph{and} risks re-incurring the
negative transfer of Sec.~\ref{sec:negtransfer}. A label-free flag that names
\emph{which} class broke is what turns this into a cheap, targeted update instead.

\paragraph{A diagnose-and-repair demonstration.} To show the loop closes, we run
a minimal proof of concept in the frozen feature space: for a flagged class we
recompute \emph{only} that class's prototype from a handful of post-drift samples
(nearest-class-mean), leaving every other class and the feature extractor
untouched (Fig.~\ref{fig:repair}). With a few dozen shots the clean teleporters
recover from near-zero recall to $\approx0.87$ (skype, the hardest case and
already partly confused before drift, recovers only partially, $0.16\to0.43$).
Because the update recomputes \emph{only} the flagged class's prototype, it cannot
by construction perturb the others, so the stable-majority Macro-$F_1$ is
unchanged ($|\Delta|\le0.001$) --- whereas CoTTA, which is free to move shared
parameters, loses $0.06$ over the same stream. The cost asymmetry is what makes
this practical: the monitor is label-free, and the fix needs a few labels on a
\emph{single} class. We report this as a proof of concept on a handful of classes,
not a full repair system, to show the diagnosis is actionable.

\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/fig_perclass_before_after_w16.png}}
\caption{Per-class diagnose-and-repair (Week-16, frozen features). Recomputing
\emph{only} the flagged class's nearest-class-mean prototype from a $k$-shot

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We have shown that the smooth accuracy decay of an encrypted-traffic
classifier is an illusion. Drift in this setting is discrete and per-class: the
macroscopic curve is a documented measurement-sensor step plus a long tail of
asynchronous per-class teleportations, not a continuous global shift. Because the
shift is local, the continuity assumption that UDA and CTTA rely on is
mis-specified here, and global adaptation does not help: gradient methods induce
negative transfer (severe at small batch, mild but still negative at the fair
operating point) and even best-case adversarial alignment yields no forward
benefit, while the no-gradient AdaBN control does no harm --- pinning the damage on
global parameter updates rather than on adaptation per se.

The monitor's contribution is robustness. The BBSE-corrected
entropy residual is a two-sided instrument: it preserves accurate, label-free tracking of hidden
$F_1$ (clean-regime correlation $\rho=-0.988$) while sharply
cutting false alarms under benign viral volume spikes (from 25.0\% to 4.6\% at
95\% detection on the Week-1 stream, with the ordering reproduced within the clean
Week-16 regime). Its two-sided mechanism --- robust to label shift, responsive to
covariate shift --- lets it localize \emph{which} application broke without any
labels, and our diagnose-and-repair demonstration shows that flag is actionable:
a per-class prototype recapture restores broken classes without disturbing the
stable majority.

Three directions remain open. First, stronger or doubly-robust estimators may
widen the robustness margin further
\cite{azizzadenesheli2019rlls,garg2020mlls,alexandari2020mlls,lee2023doubly,ziegler2023bayesian}.
Second, a full per-class few-shot class-incremental repair system, beyond the
frozen-feature proof of concept reported here, is the natural follow-up
\cite{rebuffi2017icarl,kirkpatrick2017ewc,li2017lwf,tao2020fscil}. Third, an online
version of the monitor remains open: our setting is the mirror image of online
label shift --- those methods correct a drifting $P(y)$ under a fixed
$P(x\mid y)$, whereas we cancel $P(y)$ in order to act precisely when $P(x\mid y)$
breaks --- and turning our per-window detector into a streaming, regret-bounded one
is a promising direction \cite{bai2022online,baby2023online}.

\section{Attribution Details for the eset-edtd Case Study}
\label{app:shap}
To localize the eset-edtd shift inside the model, we attribute the latent drift
back to the raw inputs with SHAP \cite{lundberg2017shap} (Fig.~\ref{fig:shap}). We
wrap the frozen Week-16 encoder so its scalar output is the embedding's distance
from the Week-16 centroid, then run a GradientExplainer over the PPI
inter-packet-time, direction, and size sequences plus the 44 flow statistics. The
week-17 jump is dominated by the PPI payload-\emph{size} sequence
(mean$|$SHAP$|=4.38$), followed by per-packet \emph{direction} ($1.50$) and
inter-packet timing ($0.49$); flowstats contribute only marginally. A
complementary check agrees: a gradient-boosted domain classifier trained to
separate the pre-cliff (weeks 14--16) from the post-cliff (weeks 17--19) regime on
interpretable per-flow features reaches AUC~$0.92$, and its top signed driver is
reverse-direction volume (BYTES\_REV falls $5578\!\to\!3551$), i.e.\ less
server-to-client payload after the cliff. Both point to a discrete change in the
packet-level size/direction signature, not a change in volume.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/fig_shap_eset_edtd.png}}
\caption{Encoder SHAP \cite{lundberg2017shap} for eset-edtd: inputs driving the
frozen Week-16 latent drift at the $\approx$wk17 cliff (objective = embedding
distance from the Week-16 centroid). The shift is dominated by the PPI
payload-size sequence, then per-packet direction and inter-packet timing;
flowstats contribute marginally. This is feature \emph{attribution} of where the
model's representation moved, not a causal claim about its cause.}
\label{fig:shap}
\end{figure}
